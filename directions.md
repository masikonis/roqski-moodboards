# Moodboard directions

## Roquemore Skierski

---

## How to read this

Four interpretations of the approved brand direction. All four share the same constraints: dark teal (#0B3030) and burnt orange (#CD4A16) on white, no containers or bordered boxes, substance-first design, elegant motion. The differences are in personality, not brand compliance.

Each direction should feel like the same firm. Side by side, a viewer should recognize the same brand but prefer one over another based on how it feels.

Pick two. The final design lives in the blend between them.

---

## 1. Still Water

A high-contrast modern serif sets the tone — tall letterforms with visible thick-thin stroke variation, the kind you'd see in GT Sectra or Editorial New. Large scale. Generous margins. The screen is mostly white, the type is mostly teal, and orange barely appears. When it does, it means something: a case result figure, an active link, a hover state. Content eases into view slowly, as though it was already there and you scrolled into it.

**What separates it:** Restraint is the whole move. Where the other directions build presence through color or structural complexity, this one builds it through what's left out. The whitespace isn't empty. It's the firm's confidence made spatial.

**Typography.** High-contrast modern serif for display — sharp wedge serifs, visible stroke modulation. Something with editorial precision rather than warmth. Body text in a neutral grotesque at comfortable reading size, generously leaded. Hierarchy is almost entirely size-driven: the heading is large, the body is moderate, and nothing in between competes.

**Color behavior.** Teal is the primary text color, used at near-black intensity. White dominates the ground. Orange appears only at decision points. Squint and the page is almost monochrome.

**Motion.** Content fades in slowly — 600-800ms — with slight upward drift. Nothing staggers. Everything arrives at the same unhurried pace. Hover states are tonal shifts (teal to orange on links) rather than movement.

**Line quality.** Hairline horizontal rules (0.5px, warm gray), used sparingly to mark major section breaks. More felt than seen.

**Depth on demand.** Expandable sections open with a slow height animation and a subtle fade. The transition feels like a drawer sliding open. When closed, there's no hint of what's underneath.

## 2. Counsel

A humanist sans-serif — the kind with open apertures and enough stroke variation to feel like a person set it, not a system. Founders Grotesk or National, something in that range. Moderate type scale. Hierarchy through weight shifts and spatial proximity, not dramatic size jumps. More orange in the palette than the other directions, but not as blocks: as the color of inline links, thin horizontal rules, highlighted figures. A recurring thread rather than a statement.

**What separates it:** Temperature. This is the warmest the brand can go without softening into something casual. The humanist type carries approachability that geometrics and serifs can't. It reads as professional but not institutional. Orange being more present adds energy without volume.

**Typography.** Humanist sans for both display and body — a family with enough weights to build hierarchy through bold/medium/regular shifts. Open apertures, slightly rounded terminals. The kind of face that works at 16px body and 48px heading without feeling like two different personalities. No dramatic scale contrast; weight and spacing do the differentiation.

**Color behavior.** Teal remains the dominant text color, but orange runs through the experience: inline links, thin rules between sections, key figures. The palette reads warmer overall even though the same two colors are in play. Roughly 70/20/10 teal/white/orange presence.

**Motion.** Content arrives in reading order with gentle staggering — each element offset by 80-100ms from the last, creating a natural scanning rhythm. The difference between a curtain rising (Still Water) and someone dealing cards. Hover states have a slight scale bump on interactive elements.

**Line quality.** Medium-weight rules (1-1.5px) in teal, more frequent than in Still Water. They act as a visible rhythm running through the page. The rules carry more structural weight here because the type isn't doing it through sheer size.

**Depth on demand.** Expandable sections open quickly and responsively (about 300ms). Content slides down and fades in simultaneously. A subtle orange accent on the trigger element signals interactivity. You asked, here it is.

## 3. Bedrock

The surface gives you almost nothing. A clean grotesque (Söhne or Akkurat), moderate sizing, minimal color, structured whitespace. It looks simple — maybe even plain. Then you interact with it. An expandable section opens to reveal a detailed case history. A hover state surfaces a team member's courtroom experience. A scroll trigger brings in a data point you didn't expect.

The personality of this direction is the gap between what you see at rest and what unfolds when you engage.

**What separates it:** Every other direction puts its personality on the surface through type, color, space, or line work. This one puts it behind interaction. "Depth on demand" is the entire thesis. The site feels like the firm itself: understated until you need it, then surprisingly deep. The "modern infrastructure" principle shows up not as visual style but as felt responsiveness — things happen fast, transitions are smooth, the technology is invisible but obviously good.

**Typography.** A well-crafted grotesque — neutral enough to stay out of the way, refined enough to hold up at reading length. The display/body distinction is minimal; almost a single-size system with weight and case doing the work. The typography doesn't want attention here. It wants to be comfortable to read while the interaction design carries the personality.

**Color behavior.** Restrained at rest. Teal text on white, almost no orange visible on the static page. Orange activates on interaction — the color of the expanded state, the hovered state, the revealed content. Color is a reward for engagement.

**Motion.** Two speeds. Surface transitions are quick and confident (200-300ms, minimal easing). Revealed content — the material inside expandable sections, data that appears on scroll — arrives with more deliberation (400-500ms, gentle ease-out). The contrast between the fast surface and the measured reveals is the rhythmic signature.

**Line quality.** Minimal. Whitespace handles most of the separation. When rules appear, they're functional: a single 1px teal line marks where interactive depth begins. The absence of visual structure on the surface is the point.

**Depth on demand.** This is where the direction lives. Expandable sections are generous — when they open, they open fully, with well-typeset content, secondary hierarchy, and even nested expandables. The expand animation has a slight spring (not bouncy, just alive). Closed sections show a one-line summary and nothing else. The contrast between the sparse closed state and the rich open state is dramatic and intentional.

## 4. Folio

The site looks like nothing else in the legal industry because it took the "no containers" constraint and made it the whole identity. Bold typographic sectioning — large, uppercase labels in teal with generous letter-spacing — and heavy horizontal rules as the dominant visual element. Content flows vertically without boxes, cards, or containment of any kind. The rhythm comes from the rules and the type hierarchy alone, closer to Swiss graphic design or a well-typeset legal brief than to other law firm websites.

**What separates it:** Material quality. The other three directions are recognizable as modern websites that happen to avoid containers. This one feels closer to a different medium — a printed brief or a Vignelli-era annual report that happens to be interactive. The heavy horizontal rules are the design language, not dividers. The typography does the structural work that boxes and cards do on other sites. You notice the lines and the type before anything else.

**Typography.** A transitional serif with real weight for headings — solid, not delicate. Something like Tiempos Headline or Freight Display at medium weight, set in uppercase for section labels with generous letter-spacing. Body text in a clean, readable sans-serif. The contrast between the weighted serif headings and the functional sans body is the primary hierarchy mechanism. Type sizes vary more here than in the other directions because the type is doing more structural work.

**Color behavior.** Teal is used as a section ground color in specific moments — not as a container, but as a full-width color field behind a heading or a rule. Orange appears on evidence: case result numbers, years of experience, court names. The color says "look at this proof."

**Motion.** Content reveals in reading order within each section, with clear pauses between groups. Horizontal rules draw on from left to right (about 400ms) as each section enters view. Subtle but distinctive — a signature that only this direction has.

**Line quality.** Heavy. 2-3px rules in dark teal, used frequently. Some run full-width, others partial-width for asymmetric visual interest. The rules have enough presence to function as architectural elements. They're what you remember about the page.

**Depth on demand.** Expandable sections are marked by a lighter, thinner rule — a visual downshift. When opened, the content inside uses the body sans exclusively (no serif headings), creating a clear typographic distinction between surface and depth. The expand animation is a clean slide with no fade. Direct, like turning to the next page.
